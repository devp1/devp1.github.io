ALS Communicator

One of my most prominent projects lies in a low-cost, mobile, standalone AAC communicator that allows patients who suffer from ALS or other impairments to communicate optically. The objective is to enable patients to communicate their basic needs in any setting without the need of complex devices or software.


Using eye tracking technology combined with Amazon Alexa’s artificial intelligence kit, I am working to create a device that allows patients to “type” letters, words, and sentences with eye movements that are as simple as looking up and looking down. When the user is done writing, the communicator can convert the text to speech, SMS, e-mail, or voice messaging.



The vision for the device:



My colleagues and I have developed a prototype for the communicator, which sets out to achieve these specific goals in clinical trials:

  • High output speech rate: The device should aim for a speech rate as close to normal human speech as possible, but achieve at least 30 words-per-minute (60 WPM is the speed achieved by a good typist)
  • Low cost: The device should cost as little as possible; our goal is to achieve a cost-per- unit of under $1,000. In contrast, current commercial devices cost tens of thousands of dollars.
  • Everyday usage: The device is lightweight and functions unobtrusively, attaching seamlessly to a pair of glasses.
  • Global: The device should be able to generate text and human comprehensible speech in multiple languages.

Haptic Glove Communicator

*Because the project is patent-pending, I am not at liberty to discuss all aspects of the Haptic Glove.

To facilitate communication with patients who are blind, deaf, or both, I am co-developing a glove-communicator that utilizes a new alphabet system based on vibrational pulses running through the patient’s fingers.

Inspired from the tap-code system that was developed by American POWs in the Hanoi Hilton, the Haptic Glove utilizes a series of two vibrational pulses that run through a patient’s fingers and wrist. Each set of two pulses designates a specific letter or character, and the “alphabet” was designed to be learned as a matrix of characters.


Tap Code used in the Hanoi Hilton:

The alphabet can be visualized as a matrix, wherein one walks down the rows first to the row with the desired character. Then, that row’s columns are being traversed to the desired character.
The Tap code used in the Hanoi Hilton was communicated with taps or knocks. To type the character H, first 2 knocks were made to go to the 2nd row and then 4 knocks to go to the 4th column: “H”.


The Haptic Glove’s Tap Code:

For the Haptic Glove (display or typing), first vibrating, tapping, or touching the index finger would select the 2nd row, then vibrating, tapping or touching the ring finger would select “H.”
The haptic glove has reading and writing methods:

  • Six haptic/vibration motors, one on each finger and one on the wrist are the “display” allowing the user to read from the connected system.
  • Six (capacitive) touch sensors, one on each finger and one on the wrist, allow the user to communicate with the connected system. This method requires 2 handed usage.
Similarly, five tap sensors - one on each finger - allow the user to communicate with the connected system.


My Current Goals:

  • Seeking the most effective method to teach new patients the Haptic Glove’s form of communication.
  • Reviewing constraints and challenges for blind, deaf, and blind/deaf patients separately to determine if a solution can be applicable across these three clusters or if separate solutions need to be developed.
